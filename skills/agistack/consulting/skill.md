# Consulting

## The Paradox at the Center of the Profession

The consultant's value is supposed to be expertise. They know things the client does not know, have seen patterns the client has not seen, can solve problems the client cannot solve alone. This is the premise of the engagement and the justification for the fee.

The paradox is that the most common reason consulting engagements fail has nothing to do with expertise. The diagnosis was accurate. The recommendation was sound. The deliverable was professionally produced. And yet the client did not implement, or implemented poorly, or implemented and then quietly returned to the previous way of doing things, and the problem that was supposed to be solved persists.

The expertise was present. Something else was missing.

What was missing was almost always one of three things: a shared understanding of what problem was actually being solved, a client who was genuinely committed to changing rather than genuinely committed to having hired someone to look at the situation, or a recommendation that was correct in theory and unworkable in the specific organizational reality where it needed to land.

This skill is built around avoiding all three failures — not just delivering good work, but delivering work that actually changes things.

## Scoping Is Where Engagements Are Won or Lost

The presenting problem is rarely the real problem. The client who says they need a new marketing strategy may actually need clarity on who they are selling to. The client who says they need help with team performance may actually need a decision about a person they have been avoiding making. The client who says they need a process improvement may actually need permission to stop doing something that stopped making sense years ago.

Accepting the presenting problem at face value and building an engagement around solving it is the fastest path to a technically correct deliverable that does not help anyone.

The skill helps you scope engagements by distinguishing the presenting problem from the underlying one. It provides frameworks for the discovery conversations that surface what is actually going on — the questions that invite honest answers rather than rehearsed ones, the silences worth holding, the moments when what is not said is more revealing than what is.

From this foundation it helps you define the engagement clearly: what success looks like in specific terms that both parties can evaluate, what is in scope and what is explicitly not, what the client will need to contribute for the work to succeed, and what the realistic timeline looks like given the complexity of what is actually being addressed.

A well-scoped engagement is easier to deliver, easier to evaluate, and easier to extend. A poorly scoped one creates the conditions for every problem that follows.

## Proposals That Reflect the Conversation

A proposal is not a brochure. It is a document that demonstrates you understood what you heard well enough to design a response that fits the actual situation rather than the generic version of it.

The proposals that win are the ones that make the client feel understood before a single deliverable is described. The executive summary that names the real problem — not the presenting problem, the real one — in language the client would use themselves. The approach section that explains not just what you will do but why you will do it in that sequence given the specific context. The investment section that frames the fee in terms of the value at stake rather than the hours to be spent.

The skill writes proposals that work this way. It takes the context from your scoping conversations and builds a document that reflects genuine understanding of the client's situation, a credible plan for addressing it, and a clear articulation of what the engagement will produce.

It also covers the mechanics that matter: the terms that protect both parties, the assumptions that need to be explicit rather than implied, the change order process that prevents scope creep from becoming a relationship problem, and the acceptance criteria that allow both parties to know when the work is done.

## Pricing as Strategy

Most consultants price by the hour because it feels safe. The client pays for the time spent. The risk of underestimating the work is minimized. The relationship between effort and compensation is direct and legible.

The problem is that hourly pricing creates a misalignment that sophisticated clients recognize and resent. The consultant who is efficient and experienced produces the answer faster than the consultant who is slower and less certain. The hourly model penalizes competence and rewards inefficiency. It also caps the consultant's income at their available hours, which is the wrong constraint for someone whose value comes from knowledge rather than time.

The skill helps you price in ways that reflect the value of the outcome rather than the cost of the input. It covers the full range of pricing approaches — project fees, retainers, value-based arrangements, hybrid structures — and helps you choose the approach that fits the specific engagement and the specific client relationship. It helps you anchor price conversations to the value at stake rather than the hours to be logged, which changes the nature of the negotiation entirely.

For clients who push back on fees, it helps you respond from a position of clarity about what you provide rather than a position of defensiveness about what you charge.

## Structuring Findings for Impact

The findings of a consulting engagement are worth nothing if they are not understood, believed, and acted upon by the people who need to act on them.

Most consulting deliverables are organized around the logic of the analysis — the methodology, the data, the findings, the implications, the recommendations, in that order. This is the order that makes sense to the analyst. It is not the order that produces action in an organization.

The skill structures findings using the principle that the recommendation comes first. The decision maker needs to know what you are recommending before they can evaluate the evidence you are about to present. Leading with the recommendation and then building the case for it — rather than building the case and then arriving at the recommendation — respects the reader's intelligence and matches how decisions are actually made.

For each finding it helps you calibrate the level of detail to the audience. The executive summary that stands alone. The supporting analysis available for the people who want to stress-test the conclusion. The implementation guidance that makes acting on the recommendation as straightforward as possible.

## When Client Relationships Get Difficult

Scope creep. Delayed feedback that stalls the engagement. A stakeholder who was not in the room when the engagement was designed and does not share the priorities of the person who hired you. A deliverable that lands differently than expected. A client who is not implementing what was agreed.

These situations are not exceptional. They are the normal texture of consulting work, and how you navigate them determines whether an engagement ends with a satisfied client who refers you or a difficult relationship that both parties are glad to have behind them.

The skill helps you navigate each of these situations with the directness and care they require. The scope creep conversation that addresses the pattern without damaging the relationship. The feedback conversation that unsticks a stalled engagement without making the client feel blamed for the delay. The stakeholder management approach that brings a skeptic into the work rather than working around them. The difficult conversation about implementation that holds the client accountable without making them defensive.

The underlying principle in all of these is the same: the client hired you because they trust your judgment. Using that judgment to name what is happening and propose a path forward — even when what is happening is uncomfortable — is what they are paying for.

## Delivering Work That Generates Referrals

The economics of a consulting practice built on referrals are fundamentally different from the economics of a practice built on outbound business development. Referred clients arrive with trust already established. The sales cycle is shorter. The fee conversation is easier. The engagement tends to start with more goodwill and produce better outcomes because the foundation is stronger.

Referrals are not generated by good work alone. They are generated by clients who felt that you understood their situation, cared about their outcome, and delivered something that made a difference they noticed — and who trust you enough with their reputation to send someone else to you.

The skill helps you build this kind of client relationship deliberately. The check-ins during an engagement that surface concerns before they become problems. The closing conversation that captures what worked and what the client would do differently, which is information that improves the next engagement and signals that you are someone who takes improvement seriously. The follow-up after the engagement ends that maintains the relationship without being transactional about it.

The best referral is one the client makes without being asked because recommending you feels like doing their colleague a favor. The skill helps you deliver work that earns that kind of recommendation.

## A Note on Independence

The consultant's value depends on their independence. The moment you tell a client what they want to hear rather than what you actually believe, you have become something other than a consultant. You have become an expensive way for them to hear their own opinions confirmed.

Clients hire consultants for their judgment. Delivering that judgment honestly — including when it is uncomfortable, including when it contradicts what the client hoped to hear, including when it requires saying something that might cost you the next engagement — is the foundation of the profession.

This skill is built on that foundation. It helps you deliver honest work clearly, diplomatically, and effectively. The honesty is not optional. The diplomacy and effectiveness are where the skill comes in.
